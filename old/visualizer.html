These visualizers take sound input and transform it into a graphical output.

They were created using Quartz Composer. The embedded videos on this page are recordings of the visualizers in action. Unfortunately, the videos do not themselves respond to sound. Click the button under each video to download the original interactive Quartz composition. (Required to play: Mac OSX and Quicktime Player; Required to play w/ audio input: Mac OSX, Quartz Composer, and microphone.

The visualizers were originally presented as a large wall projection in an art gallery setting. The sounds that visitors made were picked up by a hidden microphone.

Art & code

For this exhibition, I wanted to display work that was a collaboration between me, machine, and viewer.

Code is a fascinating artistic partner because of its transformative power: It can take input, rapidly and precisely apply some sort of algorithm, and produce output. Working with this process produces some unexpected, beautiful results.

Designing interaction

I'm interested in how people interact with computers, and how feedback sustains or terminates the interaction. I wanted to explore how these interactions can be engineered.

Sound to imagery

I chose on sound as the input mechanism because can be understood by a computer without expensive technology, and it's something almost everyone can produce and control.

I decided to convert the digitized sounds to visual output. Imagery can be incredibly powerful to me, and, I thought, others. I hoped the positive feedback from an interesting computer visualization would encourage visitors to continue interacting with the installation.

Artist Statement

When I make art, I design systems.

I choose where to introduce structure, and where to allow disorder. Computer code is my creative partner. Constructing a system requires a series of decisions. These decisions might be considered

VARIABLES,

the elements of the system that have the potential for variation. Random variables are unpredictable, volatile, and surprising. Meaning is created when the variables are forced to conform to a design. So, in order to bring sense to the system,

PARAMETERS

are set to limit the variables. What range of values are permitted? Perhaps a data-stream, a random-number algorithm, or a human action provides the data for the variable. The parameters configure the input to the system. When the

PROGRAM

is executed, the input produces an output. Programs can take the data from variables and transform it into any conceivable composition or structure. The nature of this transformation depends on the artist’s design. Change and pattern might be introduced through a computer processor’s innate ability to perform many flawless repetitions.

You are a variable in the system. Go ahead, clap your hands.
